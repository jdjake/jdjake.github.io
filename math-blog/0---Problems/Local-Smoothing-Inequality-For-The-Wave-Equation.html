Local Smoothing Inequality For The Wave Equation

In 1980, Akihiko Miyachi and Juan Carlos Peral separately proved that the half-wave propagator on is bounded from to for . In 1991, Andreas Seeger, Christopher D. Sogge, and Elias M. Stein extended this result to the study of more general half-wave propagators with respect to Riemannian metrics on a smooth manifold, and also obtain analogous results for a more general family of Fourier Integral Operators.

In 1991, Sogge noticed that the smoothness required of can be reduced if one averages over a time interval, proving a quantitative improvement and using it to obtain a generalization of Jean Bourgain’s Circular Maximal Theorem. It is natural to conjecture that the same conjecture may also hold for half-wave propagators associated with more general Riemannian metrics. But in 1997 William P. In 2000, Thomas Wolff introduced the first theory of decoupling inequalities for wave packets in order to prove the local smoothing conjecture in where is very large. This was sufficient to obtain results in geometric measure theory involving the Hausdorff dimension of sets which contain a 1-dimensional family of circles. In 2015, Bourgain and Ciprian Demeter proved the sharpest family of decoupling inequalities for the paraboloid and light cone, and as a consequence proved the local smoothing conjecture in Euclidean Space for . In 2020, David Beltran, Jonathan Hickman, and Sogge adapted this approach to the variable coefficient setting, obtaining the required decoupling inequalities for Fourier integral operators and proving the local smoothing conjecture in the Riemannian setting in the same range as Bourgain and Demeter.

Using a refined decoupling estimate, Shengwen Gan, Danqing He, Xiaochun Li and Shukun Wu were able to obtain the local smoothing conjecture on Riemannian manifolds for when is odd (the sharpest possible estimates in the general setting), and when , where when is even.

In 2020, Larry Guth, Hong Wang, and Ruixiang Zhang completely resolved the local smoothing conjecture in the Euclidean plane by establishing a reverse square-function estimate for the wave propagators. This method was quickly adapted to the setting of two-dimensional Riemannian manifolds in a 2023 paper of Chuanwei Gao, Bochen Liu, Changxing Miao, and Yakun Xi.
