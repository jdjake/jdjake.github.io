Attributes

Player Characters (PCs) have three Attributes:

  • Strength (STR): Used for Saves requiring physical power, like lifting gates, bending bars, resisting poison. STR is damaged by wounds, scrapes, cuts, or bruises. Your Character is dead if their STR drops to 0.
  • Dexterity (DEX): Used for Saves requiring poise, speed, and reflexes, such as dodging, climbing, sneaking, or balancing. DEX is damaged by sprains, cramps, muscle aches, poisons, and shocks. Your Character is paralyzed if their DEX drops to 0.
  • Willpower (WIL): Used for Saves requiring confidence, discipline, leadership, and connection to the greater universe, such as provoking, deceiving, or manipulating spells. WIL is damaged by fear, psychic attacks, low morale, and extreme mental strain. Your Character is mentally broken if their WIL drops to 0.

Attributes are not universal descripters of a character’s abilities. A character with low STR is not necessarily hopelessly weak, but rather in risky, tense situations their strength is more likely to fail them.

Hit Protection (HP)

Hit Protection (HP) reflects an ability to avoid serious damage in combat.

  • This measurement does not indicate a character’s health or fortitude, nor does it represent lasting damage to a character, as it is relatively easy to recover. See Healing & Recovery.
  • Hit Protection only applies in combat. If a PC takes damage outside of combat, it should be taken from an Attribute, typically STR.

Resolution Systems

Saves & Risk

A Save is a roll to avoid negative outcomes thrust upon the players outside of their volition. Saves are typically used for moments of split-second decisions, when something suddenly happens to a character.

  • Characters roll a d20 and compare the results to the appropriate attribute. If they roll equal to or under that attribute, they succeed. Otherwise, they fail. A 1 is always a success, and a 20 is always a failure.
  • Characters should be universally encouraged to plot, scheme, prepare, and manipulate a situation to prevent die rolls. After all, a dice roll means an opportunity of failure.
  • If two opponents are each trying to overcome the other, whoever is most at risk should save. In the case this cannot be decided, make a Contested Roll. Both Characters make a roll in a relevant attribute, and the highest successful roll wins. In the case that both rolls fail, the situation gets worse for both participants.
  • If two characters need to take an action together, whoever is most at risk should save (usually the character with the lowest relevant Attribute).

Time, Gear, & Skill

When a character consciously performs an Action that is neither impossible, nor without chance of failure, use the Time, Gear & Skill system:

  • If a Character is not explicitly in a rush, and they have time to calmly assess a situation and pick a best course of action, then they have Time.
  • If a Character has gear, equipment, or environmental benefits that outweigh the hindrences from such, then they have Gear.
  • If a Character has explicit skills or background specialization in a task, then they have Skill.

If a Character has Time, Gear, and Skill, then they automatically succeed in an Action, with no need to roll. If a Character only has one, or none of the three, then they automatically fail, with no need to roll. If Characters have two of the three, roll 1d6:

  • On a 1, they fail at a Sever Cost, and the situation changes.
  • On a 2-3, they Succeed at a Severe Cost (an Item breaks, they gain Fatigue and take damage, etc).
  • On a 4-6 they Succeed.

Consider the following examples:

When Lockpicking, a character has Time if they have ten minutes to inspect the lock and fiddle with it, Gear if they have lockpicks, and Skill if they are a thief, or if they have 15+ DEX.

When Sneaking, a character has Time if they’re not being actively chased, Gear if they’re wearing soft-soled boots (and no heavy armor, or are carrying less than 5 items), and Skill if they’re trained to be stealthy, or have 15+ DEX.

When Smashing a Door Open, a character has Time if they’re not actively under attack, Gear if they have an axe, or a crowbar, and Skill if they were once a firefighter.

When Climbing a Wall, a character has Time if they’re not being chased, Gear if they’ve got a rope and pitons (and no heavy armor), and Skill if they were once an Acrobat.

When Plotting an escape route through the city, a party have Time if they’re planning it well in advance, Gear if they have pencil and paper to note down guard routes, and good connection with the locals, and Skill if they’re a thief, or they have good familiarity with the city.

The Die of Fate

When a Player asks a question that their Character would be able to determine the answer to, or when the fiction demands an answer, the Steward first consults their prepared material. If that material, or the current state of the Fictional World, suggests a likely answer, the Steward should use this to answer the Players question.

In other situations, the Steward may answer in one or two ways:

  • If the answer does not significantly affect the situation, Stewards should simply invent an answer - perhaps colaboratively with other Players. The answer may be evocative, or set the mood, but it should not substantially harm or help the Players.

  • If the answer significantly affects the situation, Stewards may invoke the Die of Fate. The Steward asks the Player the following two questions:

    • What do they hope the answer to the question is.
      • What do they most fear the answer to the question is.

    Stewards are motivated to make that fear much worse if they feel higher stakes are necessary. The Steward then rolls a d6. On a 1-3, what the Player hopes is true. On a 4-6, what the Player most fears comes true.

Inventory

A characters Inventory represents both their literal equipment load, as well as a kind of ‘stamina bar’, through the Conditions system.

  • Inventory is abstract, dependent only on the fiction as adjudicated by the Steward.
  • Anyone filling 10 or more Inventory Slots is Encumbered, which means they cannot run, and are reduced to 0 HP.
  • Anyone carrying at most five items has a Light Load, and gains advantage on all Saves which may be advantaged by being more light on their feet.
  • Horses, mules, carts, and hirelings can make a huge difference in how much a PC can bring with them on an adventure, though these options may bring with them their own problems.
  • As a group, your crew may always assume they are carrying torches, rope, and camping equipment, and these items do not take up Inventory Slots, unless the lack of these items becomes important to the fiction. Any other items should be explicitly written in your inventory, taking up Inventory Slots if they are not Petty.

Inventory Slots

  • Most items take up one slot unless otherwise indicated.
  • Petty items do not take up any slots. Bulky items take up two slots.
  • A bag of coins worth less than £100 is Petty.

Conditions, Deprivation, and Fatigue

  • Conditions are negative effects suffed by your character. Each Condition must be placed in an Inventory Slot, which now cannot be used to store any items.
  • Some Conditions have additional effects, which apply as long as the Condition is in your inventory.
  • Conditions have a Clear requirement.
  • The most common Condition is Fatigue, Cleared after the PC is able to recuperate (i.e. after a full night’s rest in a safe spot).
  • A PC that lacks a crucial need (such as food or rest) is Deprived. Anyone Deprived for more than a day adds Fatigue, one for each day. A Deprived PC cannot recover HP, cannot recover Attributes, and cannot recover item slots from Fatigue until they get rid of their Deprived Status.
  • A PC may also be forced to add Fatigue after casting spells or due to events in the fiction.

Damage, Injury and Death

In their adventures, Characters may be damaged in several ways:

  • If a Character is damaged in combat, and the source of damage can be reasonably defended against, damage is taken from HP first, then a relevant Attribute, usually STR. See the Combat rules for more.
  • If a Character is damaged outside of combat, or the source of damage cannot be reasonably defended against (e.g. damage from a fall or from running through fire), the damage is done directly to an Attribute.

When damage is done to an Attribute, determine whether this damage could potentially be lethal, or enough to take the Character out of action. If so, the Player rolls a Critical Damage Save in the damaged attribute after taking damage.

If the Character fails the Critical Damage save, the Character takes critical damage, and cannot do anything but crawl weakly, grasping for life.

  • If given relevant aid (typically bandages) they will stabilize, bearing a Severe Wound representing their Critical Damage.
  • If left untreated, they die within the hour. NPCs and monsters that fail a Critical Damage Save are considered dead, per the Steward’s discretion.

In addition, in Combat some enemies will have special abilities or effects that are triggered when their Target fails a Critical Damage Save.

The following table can be used to determine the location of Critical Damage, adding flavor, though keep the fiction in mind:

   
d10 Location
1-5 Torso
6 Left Leg
7 Right Leg
8 Left Arm
9 Right Arm
10 Head

Some examples:

Falling from a great height onto a hard surface does d10 STR damage per 10’ fallen, and requires a Critical Damage Save.

Inhaling a certain noxious gas deals 1 STR damage per turn, with no Critical Damage Save required.

Being exposed to frigid temperatures without proper clothing deals 1 STR and 1 DEX damage per Watch, with no Critical Damage Save required.

Being injected with Night Spider venom deals d8 DEX damage, and requires a Critical Damage Save.

Catching a glimpse of something unnatural and scary deals 1 WIL damage, and does not require a Critical Damage Save.

Gazing upon the true form of an eldritch being deals d8 WIL damage, and requires a Critical Damage Save.

Wounds

Some types of damage can leave Wounds, which hinder affected adventurers more than simply dealing damage. Fire can inflict burns, electricity can inflict nerve damage, cold can inflict frostbite, monsters can inflict wounds upon dealing Critical Damage, etc. A Wound is a condition occupying a slot in the adventurer’s inventory.

Wounds come in 3 levels. Whenever a wound of the same type is gained, the player can choose to have their existing wound increase in severity or to fill in a new slot in their inventory. This allows player choice between short or long-term impact: do you suffer another light wound and heal after combat or have the old wound increase in severity without taking up another slot but possibly leading to permanent injury?

  • Light Wounds can be healed in the field if proper medicine and treatment are available. Light burns can be healed by gathering the right herbs and applying them overnight.

  • Severe Wounds require serious medical attention. Characters with Severe Wounds are considered Deprived until the wound is treated. Severe frostbite could be cured with a comfortable week-long rest in a healing temple.

  • Permanent Wounds can never be healed. Every Permanent Wound also lowers a fitting Attribute by -1 (For instance, WIL for nerve damage). While this -1 penalty can be overcome in time by training, the wound’s Inventory slot is forever lost.

Character Death

When a character dies, the player should create a new Character or take control of a Hireling. They immediately join the party in order to reduce time sat out of the game.

Healing & Recovery

Catching Your Breathe

A Short Rest takes a few minutes, and consists of taking time to sip water and converse with your crew. A Short Rest fully restores lost HP, but may leave the party exposed.

  • Water is kept in a Waterskin, which occupies 1 slot. Fully filled it has a Usage Die of d8. Whenever a character recovers HP, they roll the Waterskin’s Usage Die. On a 1 or 2, the die decreases in size (from a d8, to a d6, to a d4). If a 1-2 is rolled on a d4 Usage Die, the waterskin is empty.
  • During a rest, players are encouraged to role-play a scene that develops the fellowship between the party. Share a memory, talk about goals, ask about their history or tell them yours.
  • After the scene, Players nominate the party member whose fellowship was most brought up during the rest gain a Bond which lasts until the next time the players rest (and so only a single player has a Bond at any moment of play); if that player makes a Save they feel relates to the conversation during the last rest, they may spend their Bond to gain advantage on the roll.

Long Term Recovery

  • Bandages can stabilize a character that has taken Critical Damage, though the character will sustain Wounds.
  • Attribute Loss can be restored by a week’s rest in a Comfortable Spot. Recovery can be hastened by a healer or other appropriate source of expertise.
  • Some healing services are free, while magical or more expedient means of recovery may come at a cost.

Magic

Spellbooks

  • Spellbooks contain a single spell and take up one Inventory Slot. They cannot be easily transcribed or created; instead they are recovered from places like tombs, dungeons, and manors.
  • Spellbooks sometimes display unusual properties or limitations, such as producing a foul or unearthly smell when opened, possessing an innate intelligence, or being legible only when held in moonlight.
  • Spellbooks will attract the attention of those who seek the arcane power within, and it is considered dangerous to display them openly.

Casting Spells

  • Anyone can cast a spell by holding a Spellbook in both hands and reading its contents aloud. They must then take a Fatigue.
  • Given time and safety, PCs can enhance a spell’s impact (affecting multiple targets, increasing its power, etc.) without additional cost.
  • If the PC is Deprived or in danger (such as during combat), the Steward may require a PC to make a WIL save to avoid any ill-effects from casting the spell. Consequences of failure are on par with the intended effect and may result in additional Fatigue, the destruction of the Spellbook, injury, and even death.

Scrolls

Scrolls are similar to Spellbooks, however:

  • They are Petty.
  • They do not cause Fatigue.
  • They disappear after one use.

Relics

Relics are items imbued with magical power. They do not cause Fatigue. Relics usually have limited use.

Combat

Rounds

  • A Round is roughly ten seconds of in-game time and proceeds with each side taking Turns. Each Round starts with any PC that is able to act, followed by their opponents. Each player declares the actions of their characters, before simultaneously resolving their actions. Typically, the Steward will telegraph the actions of opponents to some degree when prompted.
  • During the first Round of combat, each PC must make a DEX Save in order to act. Special circumstances, abilities, items, or skills may negate this requirement. PCs that fail their save lose their Turn for this round.
  • After the PC’s first round, their opponents then take their turn, and the first round ends. The next round begins with the PCs taking their turn, followed by their opponents, and so on until combat has ended with one side defeated or fled.

Actions

On their Turn, a Character may move up to 40ft and take up to one Action. This can be anything reasonably done in 10 seconds, such as casting a spell, attacking, or moving for a second time. Each round, all PCs declare what they are doing before dice are rolled. If a character attempts something risky, the Steward calls for an appropriate save.

Attacking & Damage

  • Attacks in combat automatically hit. The attacker rolls their damage die and subtracts the target’s AP, then deals the remaining total to their opponent’s HP.
  • If multiple attackers target the same foe, roll all damage dice and keep the single highest result. All actions are declared before being resolved.
  • If an attack would take a PC’s HP exactly to 0, refer to the Scars table to see how they are uniquely impacted.

Attack Modifiers

  • If fighting from a position of weakness (such as through cover or with bound hands), the attack is Impaired, and the attacker must roll d4 damage regardless of their damage die. Unarmed attacks always do d4 damage.
  • If fighting from a position of advantage (such as against a helpless foe or through a daring maneuver), the attack is Enhanced, allowing the attacker to roll d12 damage instead of their normal die.
  • Attacks with the Blast quality damage all targets in the noted area, rolling separately for each affected character. This can be anything from explosions to a dragon’s breath or the impact of a meteorite. If unsure how many targets can be affected, roll the related damage die for a result.
  • If attacking with two weapons at the same time, roll both damage dice and keep the single highest result (denoted with a plus symbol, e.g. d8+d8).

Armor (AP)

  • Before calculating HP Damage, subtract the target’s Armor Protection (AP) value from the result.
  • Shields and similar armor provide a bonus to AP while worn. Some may also provide additional benefits, depending on the fiction.
  • No Character, PC or otherwise, can have more than 3 AP.

Detachments

  • Large groups of similar combatants fighting together are treated as a single Detachment. When a Detachment takes Critical Damage, it is routed or significantly weakened. When it reaches 0 STR, it is destroyed.
  • Attacks against Detachments by individuals are Impaired (excluding Blast damage). Attacks against individuals by detachments are Enhanced and deal Blast damage.

Retreat

  • Running away from a dire situation, such as combat, always requires a successful DEX Save, as well as a safe destination to run to.

Morale

  • Enemies must pass a WIL Save to avoid fleeing when they take their first casualty and again when they lose half their number. Lone foes must save when they’re reduced to 0 HP.
  • Some groups may use their leader’s WIL in place of their own.
  • Morale does not affect PCs.

Ranged Attacks

  • Ranged weapons can target any enemy near enough to see the whites of their eyes. Attacks against especially distant targets are Impaired.
  • Ammunition is not tracked unless otherwise specified.

Scars

If damage to a PC would reduce their HP to exactly 0, look up the result on the table below based on the amount of HP lost in the attack. For example, if a PC went from 3 HP to 0 HP, they would look at entry #3 (Walloped).

   
HP Lost Result
1 Lasting Scar: Roll 1d6. 1: Neck, 2: Hands, 3: Eye, 4: Chest, 5: Legs, 6: Ear. Roll 1d6. If the total is higher than your max HP, take the new result.
2 Rattling Blow: You’re disoriented and shaken. Describe how you refocus. Roll 1d6. If the total is higher than your max HP, take the new result.
3 Walloped: You’re sent flying and land flat on your face, winded. You are deprived until you rest for a few hours. Then, roll 1d6. Add that amount to your max HP.
4 Broken Limb: Roll 1d6. 1-2: Leg, 3-4: Arm, 5: Rib, 6: Skull. Once mended, roll 2d6. If the total is higher than your max HP, take the new result.
5 Diseased: You’re afflicted with a gross, uncomfortable infection. When you get over it, roll 2d6. If the total is higher than your max HP, take the new result.
6 Reorienting Head Wound: Roll 1d6. 1-2: STR, 3-4: DEX, 5-6: WIL. Roll 3d6. If the total is higher than your current attribute, take the new result.
7 Hamstrung: You can barely move until you get serious help and rest. After recovery, roll 3d6. If the total is higher than your max DEX, take the new result.
8 Deafened: You cannot hear anything until you find extraordinary aid. Regardless, make a WIL save. If you pass, increase your max WIL by 1d4.
9 Re-brained: Some hidden part of your psyche is knocked loose. Roll 3d6. If the total is higher than your max WIL, take the new result.
10 Sundered: An appendage is torn off, crippled, or useless. (The Steward will tell you which.) Then make a WIL save. If you pass, increase your max WIL by 1d6.
11 Mortal Wound: You are deprived and out of action. You die in one hour unless healed. Upon recovery, roll 2d6. Take the new result as your max HP.
12 Doomed: Death seemed ever so close, but somehow you survived. If your next save against critical damage is a fail, you die horribly. If you pass, roll 3d6. If the total is higher than your max HP, take the new result.
