Player Expectations

These rules provide guidelines for the GM and other Players, to help foster a specific play experience defined by critical thinking, engagement with the imaginary world, character development, and emergent narrative.

Roles

Chime challenges the GM to act as a neutral arbiter, portraying grounded situations and non-player characters to the other players, and providing contextual clarity to foster the other Players ability to make interesting decisions. Chime challenges the other Players to engage with the imaginary world, portray interesting characters with strong beliefs, respond to situations in clever and interesting ways, and to drive the fiction forward in interesting ways through their decisions.

Fiction First

In world context overrides mechanics and numbers. Players engage with the world by describing their character’s actions within the world, with most consequences of those actions arbitrated by the GM rather than mediated through specific rules. This is closely related to the maxim of ‘Rulings Not Rules’.

Classless

A Character’s role or skills is not defined by a particular class or attributes. Instead, a character specializes with their experience, their learning, and the equipment they choose to carry.

Danger

Characters may be powerful, but they are also vulnerable to harm in its many forms. Death is always around the corner, but it should never be random or without warning. A dagger to the throat will kill a man, regardless of their expensive armor and impressive training. This danger adds impact to a Player’s decisions and compels them to engage with the imaginary world, because their character is not superheroic enough to stand above it.

Growth

Characters are changed through in-world advancement, gaining new skills and abilities by surviving dangerous events, overcoming obstacles, and making connections with others within the imaginary world.

Player Choice

Players should always understand the reasons behind the choices they’ve made, and information about potential risks should be provided freely and frequently by the GM.

Emergent Narrative

Neither the GM nor the other Players are responsible for molding a narrative structure from the game. Players act according to beliefs, motivations, and their perception of the imaginary world, and the GM provides a lens to the imaginary world, as well as fostering the Player’s ability to make interesting decisions. Narrative emerges from these interesting decisions in retrospect, just as stories emerge in real life, i.e. by filtering through a series of events to find a chain of interconnected events that have meaning to us.

Player Guidelines

Agency

  • Attributes are tools to engage with the imaginary world, but do not define your character.
  • Engage with the imaginary world as if you existed within it.
  • Be creative with your intuitions, items, and connections. Try and make the GM say “I didn’t even think of that!”.
  • Keep games focused by coming to a game with a plan of what you want to accomplish in a session already discussed with other players.

Teamwork

  • Trust each other to engage with the shared setting, to help others with the goals of their characters and of the overall team. Work together as a team.
  • Seek consensus from other players before barreling forward.
  • Stay on the same page about goals and limits, respecting each other and accomplishing more as a group than alone.
  • Let the relationships between your characters grow and change.

Engage With The World

  • Asking questions, listening to details, and engaging with the world is more useful than any stats, items, or skills you have.
  • Take the GM’s description without suspicion, but do not shy away from scrutinizing your environment for more information.
  • There is no correct solution to any problem, nor any correct path forward.

Talking

  • Treat NPCs as if they were real people, and rely on your curiosity to safely gain information and solve problems.
  • You’ll find that most people are interesting and will want to talk things through before getting violent.

Caution

  • Think of ways to avoid your obstacles through reconnaissance, subtlety, and information gathering.
  • Fighting is a choice, and rarely a wise one; consider whether violence is the best way to achieve your goals.
  • Stack the odds in your favor as much as possible, and always keep retreat as an option when a situation becomes unfavorable.

Ambition

  • Set goals for your characters, and use your meager means to accomplish them.
  • Embody characters with beliefs that distinguish them from the general party. Set personal goals to develop your character further.
  • Actively move situations forward when they stall, plan goals for sessions in advance to keep games focused.
  • Play to find out what happens rather than anticipating plot threads or expecting certain outcomes.

GM Guidelines

Information

  • Provide useful information in response to the explorations of the Players. Respond to smart questions with helpful, direct, consistent answers.
  • Players do not need to roll dice to understand their circumstances.

Difficulty

  • If something the players want to do is sincerely impossible, no roll will allow them to do it.
  • If a player leverages a situation in a sensible, risk-free manner, let the consequences happen without a roll.

Preparation

  • Consider the Blorb principles.
  • Use random tables and generators to develop situations, not stories or plots.
  • NPCs remember what the PCs say and do, and how they affect the world.
  • NPCs don’t want to die. Infuse their own self-interest and will to live into every personality.

Narrative Focus

  • Emergent play matters more than a planned outcome.
  • Give the players opportunities to facilitate improvement and specialization, and encourage them to foster personal goals.
  • Pay attention to the desires and beliefs of the players, then put realistic opportunities in their path.

Danger

  • The game world produces real risk of pain and death for the player characters.
  • Telegraph serious danger to players when it is present. The more dangerous, the more obvious. Put traps in plain sight.
  • Give players opportunities to solve problems and interact with the world.

Treasure

  • Treasures are specific to the environment from where it is recovered. They tell a story.
  • Treasure is highly valuable, almost always Bulky, and rarely useful beyond its worth and prestige.
  • Relics are not Treasure, though they are useful and interesting.
  • Use Treasure as a lure to exotic locations protected of intimidating foes.

Choice

  • Give players solid choices to force outcomes when situations lull.
  • Ensure that the player character’s actions are impactful, leaving a mark on the imaginary world.
